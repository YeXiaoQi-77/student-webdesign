EFFECTS OF GLOBAL WARMING

Earth's temperature has risen by about 1.1°C since the late 19th century, mostly due to greenhouse gas emissions from human activity. This rise increases the frequency of extreme heat events, disrupts agriculture, and intensifies droughts, posing threats to ecosystems and public health.

The polar regions are warming more than twice as fast as the global average, causing glaciers and ice sheets to melt rapidly. This meltwater contributes to rising sea levels, threatening millions living in coastal communities with flooding, erosion, and saltwater intrusion.

Climate change increases the intensity and frequency of hurricanes, heatwaves, wildfires, and floods. Warming oceans fuel stronger storms, while disrupted jet streams can prolong extreme weather patterns, overwhelming infrastructure and endangering lives.

As temperatures rise, many species struggle to adapt or migrate fast enough. Coral reefs are bleaching, forests face new pests, and animal migrations are shifting. This imbalance threatens food chains, biodiversity, and ecosystem stability globally.
